Pulmonary

Definition

An illness of the lungs called emphysema can be identified by damage to the alveoli, or lung air sacs. Emphysema causes the creation of bigger air gaps rather than numerous little ones because the inner walls of the air sacs weaken and finally burst. Because of this damage, there is less surface area in the lungs accessible for gas exchange, which makes it more difficult for oxygen to enter the circulation. Chronic bronchitis, one of the conditions that comprise chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), is frequently linked to emphysema.

How and When it Occurs

Smoking is the main cause of emphysema, while there are other contributing aspects as well. Emphysema develops when lung tissue is destroyed by long-term exposure to cigarette smoke. Chronic occupational exposure to metal fumes, organic dusts, diesel exhaust fumes, chemical gases or vapors, and mineral dusts are additional important risk factors. Moreover, emphysema can also develop due to hereditary causes such as alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD), especially in those under the age of thirty. AAT protein synthesis that is aberrant in AATD is a hereditary condition that causes damage to the sensitive gas exchange zone of the lungs due to neutrophil elastase. This damage can result in emphysema. In addition to causing pulmonary vascular damage, intravenous drug usage, especially with drugs containing insoluble fillers such as cornstarch, cotton fibers, cellulose, and talc, can also contribute to the development of emphysema. Emphysema risk factors can also include illnesses like Pneumocystis carinii infection, autoimmune deficiency syndrome, and HIV infections. In general, the genesis of emphysema may be attributed to a mix of lifestyle variables, environmental exposures, and genetic susceptibility.

Nursing Managements

1. Sustaining a clear airway:

-To aid with secretion mobilization, use a humidifier.
-Promote deliberate coughing to get rid of mucus.
-As needed, use breathing treatments and keep an eye on lung sounds.

2. Techniques for positioning and breathing:

-To enhance lung expansion, place the patient in a high Fowler posture.
-Instruct on diaphragmatic and pursed-lip breathing.

3. Observation and evaluation:

-Regularly auscultate lung sounds.
-Track ABGs to evaluate respiratory health.
-To avoid infections, visitors should be screened for infectious illnesses.

4. Energy-saving measures:

--Schedule your activities to include downtime.
-Help with daily tasks to reduce energy use.

5. Patient education:

-Inform the patient about managing their medications, making lifestyle changes, and the condition.
-Give advice on how to stay away from irritants like cigarette smoke.

6. Immunization:

-Vaccinate against pneumococcal disease and influenza to avoid respiratory infections.

7. Referrals and recovery:

-If one is available, direct the patient to a pulmonary rehabilitation program.
-Continually assist and educate people on long-term management.

The goals of nursing management for emphysema patients are to maximize pulmonary function, avoid complications, and enhance patient satisfaction by using a range of treatments based on the patient's needs and stage of the illness.
