Gastroenterology

Definition

Helicobacter Pylori, or H. Pylori is the most common causative agent of Gastritis. In the Philippines, the prevalence of H. Pylori infection is about 21% according to the study conducted in 2019 by Khodel et., al. Gastritis is a condition where an individual's stomach lining mucosa is inflamed.

The diagnostic test of this disease starts with the assessment of the healthcare provider on the symptoms and history of your health before having a suspicion of gastritis. Afterward, there will be laboratory tests to be conducted to confirm an individualâ€™s symptoms of gastritis such as blood tests, stool test,s and H. pylori breath tests. Moreover, the healthcare provider may order to have you get an X-ray of your GI tract to see if there are any ulcerations or erosions in your stomach lining. Sometimes, healthcare providers may need to do an endoscopy and biopsy to confirm gastritis; however, this is not often the case as most of the time providers detect the disorder with the aid of your lab results.

TYPES OF GASTRITIS

There are 2 common types of Gastritis which are Acute and Chronic.

Acute gastritis - refers to a brief inflammation of the stomach lining caused by stress on the gastric mucosa, leading to either hemorrhagic or non-hemorrhagic symptoms. This condition can arise from various factors like uremia, ischemia, shock, corrosive substances, medications, radiation, trauma, severe burns, sepsis, or alkaline-bile reflux. Infections such as enteroviruses can also induce a temporary episode of gastritis. Reduced gastric mucus secretion, disruption of the mucosal barrier, or diminished mucosal blood flow can contribute to acute gastritis, depending on the underlying trigger.

Chronic gastritis - can be classified into two types: atrophic and non-atrophic. The main cause of chronic gastritis is typically a Helicobacter pylori infection, which usually begins as a non-atrophic form. Without proper treatment, non-atrophic chronic gastritis can progress to atrophic forms. Autoimmune gastritis is the most common cause of atrophic chronic gastritis, although the exact cause of this condition remains uncertain. Autoimmune gastritis is characterized by persistent mononuclear inflammation often accompanied by severe atrophic gastritis, primarily affecting the corpus, and the presence of autoantibodies against parietal cells or the intrinsic factor. It is still unclear whether autoimmune gastritis is an independent condition or if an H. pylori infection triggers an autoimmune response in susceptible individuals.

How and When it Occurs

The presence of Gastritis may be caused by an infection or an immunological response of an individual. At the same time, excessive use of NSAIDs or painkillers could trigger or cause gastritis to arise. Gastritis occurs when the stomach wall lining, a barrier protecting the stomach from bacteria, is irritated or damaged.

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

1. The main symptoms of acute gastritis include:
2. Stomach pain
3. Feeling of fullness
4. Flatulence (gas)
5. Heartburn
6. Nausea and sometimes vomiting
7. Belching
8. Lack of appetite
9. Bloated belly

Nursing Managements

1. Assess the patient's condition by asking about their health history, lifestyle, and pain.

2. Administer pain management such as antacids, proton pump inhibitors, and H2 blockers to neutralize gastric acid, as ordered by the physician.

3. Monitor the patientâ€™s Fluid and Electrolyte balance. At the same time, encourage the patient to increase fluid intake or administer IV fluid as ordered to prevent dehydration.

4. Educate the patient to eat small frequent meals and avoid irritant foods such as spicy, acidic, or fried food to reduce gastric irritation.

5. Provide Patient Education on medication adherence as part of their recovery. At the same time, educate the patient on avoiding NSAIDs and alcohol consumption as these are contributing factors to gastritis.

6. Encourage patient relaxation and stress management. Referral to a counselor or psychologist could help with a patientâ€™s healing and stress management.

7. Monitor the patient regularly for pain, nausea, and vomiting.
