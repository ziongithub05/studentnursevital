Emergency

Anaphylaxis

Scenario

Sarah, a 30-year-old woman with a severe peanut allergy, accidentally ingests a peanut-containing chocolate bar at a social gathering. Within minutes, she experiences a rapid onset of symptoms indicative of an anaphylactic reaction. Her skin erupts in hives, accompanied by intense itching and redness. Swelling begins in her lips and face, making speaking difficult. Sarah also notices tightness in her chest, leading to wheezing, coughing, and a sense of breathlessness. Her heart rate escalates, causing lightheadedness and dizziness, while nausea sets in, followed by vomiting and abdominal pain. Recognizing the gravity of her situation, Sarah alerts her friend for immediate medical help and retrieves her epinephrine auto-injector.

Definition

Anaphylaxis is a severe and potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that occurs rapidly after exposure to an allergen. This type of allergic reaction is systemic, meaning it affects the entire body. Common allergens that can trigger anaphylaxis include certain foods (like peanuts, shellfish, and eggs), insect stings (such as from bees or wasps), medications (like penicillin or aspirin), and latex. During an anaphylactic reaction, the immune system releases large amounts of histamine and other chemicals, leading to a cascade of symptoms that can affect the skin, respiratory system, cardiovascular system, and gastrointestinal tract. Prompt recognition and treatment of anaphylaxis are essential to prevent serious complications such as respiratory failure, cardiovascular collapse, or death.

How it happens

Anaphylaxis is a rapid and severe allergic reaction that occurs when the immune system overreacts to an allergen. The process begins with sensitization, where the individual's immune system recognizes the allergen as harmful and produces specific IgE antibodies. Upon re-exposure to the allergen, these antibodies bind to mast cells and basophils, triggering their degranulation and the release of inflammatory mediators like histamine, leukotrienes, and cytokines. This systemic release of mediators leads to a cascade of effects throughout the body, causing characteristic symptoms of anaphylaxis. These symptoms can include skin reactions such as hives, itching, and swelling; respiratory symptoms like wheezing, coughing, and difficulty breathing; cardiovascular changes such as rapid heart rate and low blood pressure; and gastrointestinal symptoms including nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.

Nursing Managements

1. Recognition and Assessment: -Quickly recognize signs and symptoms of anaphylaxis, such as skin changes (hives, swelling), respiratory distress (wheezing, shortness of breath), cardiovascular instability (rapid heart rate, low blood pressure), and gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting).

-Obtain a detailed allergy history, including known allergens, previous reactions, and medications.

2. Airway Management:

-Ensure a patent airway and adequate oxygenation. Administer supplemental oxygen if needed.

-Be prepared to initiate advanced airway management (e.g., endotracheal intubation) if respiratory distress worsens.

3. Administer Epinephrine:

-Epinephrine is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis and should be administered promptly via intramuscular injection (auto-injector) in the outer thigh.

-Educate patients and caregivers on the proper use of epinephrine auto-injectors and the importance of carrying them at all times.

4. Fluid Resuscitation:

-Initiate intravenous access for fluid resuscitation, especially if the patient is hypotensive or in shock.

-Administer isotonic fluids (e.g., normal saline) to restore intravascular volume and improve perfusion.

5. Medication Administration:

-Administer additional medications as ordered, such as antihistamines (e.g., diphenhydramine) to alleviate allergic symptoms and corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone) to prevent delayed reactions.

-Consider bronchodilators (e.g., albuterol) for bronchospasm and nebulized medications for respiratory support.

6. Monitoring and Observations:

-Continuously monitor vital signs, including blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and temperature.

Assess the patient's level of consciousness, airway patency, and respiratory effort.

Watch for signs of improvement or deterioration, including repeat assessments of skin, respiratory, cardiovascular, and gastrointestinal status.

7. Documentation and Communication:

Document the onset and progression of anaphylaxis, interventions performed, response to treatment, and patient outcomes.

Communicate effectively with the healthcare team, including physicians, pharmacists, and other providers, to ensure coordinated care and follow-up.

8. Education and Follow-Up:

Provide education to the patient and family on allergen avoidance, recognition of early warning signs of anaphylaxis, and the importance of seeking immediate medical attention for future reactions.

Arrange follow-up appointments for further evaluation, allergy testing, and anaphylaxis action plans.
