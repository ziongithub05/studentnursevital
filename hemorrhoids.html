Gastroenterology

Definition

Hemorrhoids or known as piles are inflamed or swollen veins inside or around the anus of the lower rectum. Hemorrhoids are usually asymptomatic, thus, the prevalence of this disorder is hard to measure. Additionally, the site, presence of thrombosis, and size of the hemorrhoids shows the amount of pain that the patient need to bear, according to Mott et., al., cited by Matthews (2018).

2 MAIN TYPES OF HEMORRHOIDS:

1. External Hemorrhoids: These develop beneath the skin surrounding the anus.

2. Internal Hemorrhoids: These form within the lining of the anus and the lower rectum.

Physicians diagnose Hemorrhoids by:

1. Assessing Patient’s Symptoms

2. Physical Examination

3. Digital Rectal Exam - healthcare provider will insert a gloved, lubricated finger into your rectum to feel for any swollen veins, which could indicate the presence of hemorrhoids.

4. Anoscop - your provider will use a lighted tube called an anoscope to visually examine the lining of your anus and rectum.

5. Sigmoidoscopy- This procedure involves the use of a sigmoidoscope, a lighted tube with a camera, to view the lower part of your colon and rectum. This can be done as a flexible sigmoidoscopy or a rigid sigmoidoscopy (also called a proctoscopy).

6. In some cases, your provider may also recommend a colonoscopy to confirm the findings from the other tests or to check for any signs of colon cancer. A colonoscopy is an outpatient procedure that does require anesthesia.

How and When it Occurs

If you are experiencing external hemorrhoids, common symptoms include anal itching, the presence of firm and tender lumps near the anus, and discomfort or pain in the anal area, particularly when sitting. Excessive straining, rubbing, or cleaning around the anus can exacerbate these symptoms. Fortunately, for many individuals, external hemorrhoid symptoms typically resolve within a few days.

On the other hand, internal hemorrhoids may manifest as rectal bleeding, noticeable as bright red blood on stool, toilet paper, or in the toilet bowl after a bowel movement. In some cases, an internal hemorrhoid may protrude through the anal opening, a condition known as prolapse.

Nursing Managements

1. Assess the patient’s bowel movement, pain, anal bleeding and itching.

2. Inspect Anal Area for external or prolapsed hemorrhoids with patient consent.

3. Administer prescribed topical medications as ordered by physician.

4. Monitor laboratory tests as prescribed.

5. Encourage and monitor adequate fluid intake.

6. Encourage high fiber diet intake.

7. Administer stool softeners or laxatives as prescribed by the physician.

8. Evaluate bowel patterns, including frequency and features.

9. Evaluate bowel sounds.

10. Monitor vital signs.

11. Administer analgesics as prescribed by physician.

12. Offer warm sitz baths.

13. Evaluate Patient’s understanding of their situation and adherence to provided health education.
