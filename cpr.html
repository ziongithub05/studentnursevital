Emergency

CPR

Scenario

You're working as a nurse in a hospital when a code blue is called for a 60-year-old patient who suddenly collapsed in the hallway. Upon arrival at the scene, you find the patient unresponsive, not breathing, and without a pulse. There are no signs of trauma.

Definition

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) is an emergency procedure performed to manually maintain circulation and oxygenation in a person whose heart has stopped beating (cardiac arrest) or who is not breathing effectively. CPR involves chest compressions to pump blood through the body and rescue breaths to provide oxygen to the lungs.

How it happens

CPR is typically initiated in response to a sudden cardiac arrest or respiratory arrest. In cardiac arrest, the heart's normal electrical activity is disrupted, leading to a cessation of effective pumping action. This can result from various causes, including heart attack, drowning, suffocation, severe trauma, or drug overdose. In respiratory arrest, breathing may stop due to airway obstruction, respiratory failure, or neurological conditions.

Nursing Managements

1. Assessing the Scene: Ensure the environment is safe for both the rescuer and the victim before approaching.

2. Checking Responsiveness: Gently tap the victim and shout loudly to check for any response. If there is no response, call for help immediately (activate emergency services).

3. Opening the Airway: Tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway.

4. Checking Breathing: Look, listen, and feel for normal breathing for no more than 10 seconds. If the victim is not breathing normally, proceed with CPR.

5. Performing Chest Compressions: Place the heel of one hand on the center of the victim's chest (lower half of the breastbone or sternum), place the other hand on top, and interlock fingers. Deliver compressions at a rate of about 100 to 120 per minute (to the beat of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees). Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions.

6. Rescue Breaths (if trained): After 30 compressions, give two rescue breaths. Pinch the victim's nose, make a complete seal over their mouth, and blow until you see the chest rise.

7. Continue Cycle: Alternate between compressions and rescue breaths in a ratio of 30:2 until advanced medical help arrives or the victim shows signs of life.
