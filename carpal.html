Carpal Tunnnel Syndrome

Definition

Carpal tunnel syndrome is a medical condition characterized by manifestations such as pain, numbness, tingling, and weakness in the hand and wrist. The carpal tunnel can be likened to a passage within the wrist bones, resembling a roadway through a mountain. However, instead of accommodating vehicles, it serves as a conduit for tendons, ligaments, and nerves to reach the hand.

How and When it Occurs

Carpal tunnel syndrome develops when there is irritation or increased pressure on the median nerve traversing through the carpal tunnel. This nerve facilitates forearm movement and provides sensation to most fingers and hands. When the median nerve sustains damage or becomes compressed within the confines of the carpal tunnel, it can result in abnormal sensations in the hand and wrist. Elevated pressure on the median nerve is the underlying cause of carpal tunnel syndrome. Although the carpal tunnel accommodates all the structures passing through it, swelling or injury in one area of the wrist can lead to compression of adjacent tissues, including the median nerve. Various factors can contribute to wrist swelling or irritation, thereby predisposing individuals to carpal tunnel syndrome, including: Repetitive strain injuries, Arthritis, Sprains, Wrist fractures, Ganglion cysts.

Nursing Managements

Healthcare providers typically begin treating carpal tunnel syndrome with nonsurgical (conservative) methods. If these fail to alleviate symptoms, carpal tunnel surgery may be recommended. Nonsurgical treatments focus on modifying daily activities, providing support to the wrist, and administering medication:

Splinting: Wearing a splint, especially at night, helps maintain the wrist in a neutral position, relieving pressure on the median nerve.

Physical therapy: Strengthening wrist muscles and improving flexibility under the guidance of a physical therapist.

Ergonomic adjustments: Occupational therapists can suggest modifications to daily tasks and work environments to ensure safer and more comfortable movement. This may involve changes in posture, seating, keyboard positioning, or other ergonomic adjustments.

Medication: Over-the-counter NSAIDs or acetaminophen can be recommended to reduce inflammation and alleviate pain, although prolonged use should be supervised by a healthcare provider.

Corticosteroids: Prescription cortisone shots may be administered directly into the affected carpal tunnel to reduce inflammation.

In cases where conservative treatments are ineffective, carpal tunnel surgery, known as carpal tunnel release, may be necessary. This outpatient procedure involves making an incision in the transverse carpal ligament to enlarge the carpal tunnel, thus relieving pressure on the tendons and nerves. Patients receive postoperative instructions and can typically return home the same day.
