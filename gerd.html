Gastroenterology

Definition

WHAT IS GERD?

GERD is known as Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease. GERD is associated with acid reflux; however, it is much worse than normal acid reflux. When an occasional acid reflux becomes unmanageable or happens at least twice a week/ several weeks, that is now a chronic reflux known as GERD.

Esophagram: An esophagram is a form of X-ray examination that captures dynamic X-ray images (fluoroscopy) of the esophagus while you ingest a barium-based contrast solution.

Upper Endoscopy: During an upper endoscopy, a camera attached to a slender tube is inserted through the mouth to visualize the interior of the esophagus while the patient is sedated.

Esophageal pH Test: This diagnostic procedure measures the acidity levels in the esophagus using a small wireless receiver placed inside the esophagus during an endoscopic examination.

Esophageal Manometry: This test evaluates the muscle activity in the esophagus by utilizing pressure sensors within a nasogastric tube, helping to identify any abnormalities in the function of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) or other esophageal muscles.

How and When it Occurs

GERD or Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease occurs when there is regurgitation of stomach content to the esophagus more than usual. More likely, it develops because the lower esophageal sphincter closer to our stomach becomes weak. This sphincter closes normally preventing gastric content from going back up to the esophagus. SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

1. Backwash - A liquid flushing from the stomach to the esophagus and then to the mouth leaving a sour taste of acid.
2. Heartburn
3. Chest pain
4. Nausea and Vomiting
5. Sore throat
6. Asthma symptoms

If you have GERD or suspected to have one, the symptoms may worsen:

1. At night
2. When Lying Down
3. After a large fatty meal
4. After Bending Over
5. After Smoking and Drinking Alcohol

Nursing Managements

1. Assess the patient's health history and the frequency and duration of GERD symptoms.

2. Assess the patient’s dietary habits, lifestyle, medications, and psychosocial health to identify factors that exacerbate their GERD symptoms.

3. Perform a Physical Examination on the abdomen and chest to identify signs of GERD such as epigastric tenderness and respiratory symptoms.

4. Conduct pain assessments (PQRST).

5. Obtain 12-lead EKG as ordered.

6. Prepare and Assist the physician with endoscopy to visualize the GI tract.

7. Administer medications such as Proton Pump Inhibitors, Antacids, and Antibiotics as ordered by the physician.

8. Monitor the patient's symptoms regularly.

9. Educate the patient on lifestyle changes, diet, and avoidance of nicotine and alcohol.

10. Encourage good oral hygiene.

11. Assess the patient's adoption of health education and quality of life due to GERD.
